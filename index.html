Optical Characterization and Comparison of Photolithography Process

Texas Instruments Process Engineer Intern | Summer 2017

Texas Instruments is a global semiconductor microchip manufacturer. One of the most crucial parts and the most common step in the semiconductor manufacturing process is photolithography. During photolithography, a pattern is printed onto a semiconductor wafer to be used in other steps of building the integrated circuit. This pattern is created by applying a light sensitive chemical layer to the chip, exposing it to light through a photomask, and developing the pattern by washing aways the parts of the layer made more soluable by the light exposure. For more information about how microchips are made, including an overview of the photolithography process, check out this video made by Infineon Technologies.

As a Process Engineer in the Photolithography Manufacturing Department, I worked on three projects simultaneously to optimize processes. My favorite project involved printing two wafers utilizing tool different exposure tools and performing optical characterization of 40+ unique structures within the patterns using a scanning electron microscope. I then analyzed the critical measurements and visual analysis of these structures through focus for comparison. The goal of this project was to determine if any adjustments needed to be made to the photomask for the new type of exposure tool to account for differing light diffraction. If the patterns are similar and within normal target range, the process can be updated to utilize both the current type of exposure tool and the new type of exposure tool, reducing tool downtime and more efficienty printing this particular pattern. I presented my results to the lead engineer of this pattern process and upper management of the smiconductor fab. While further analysis needed to be obtained for structures in memory, when I left the internship, results looked promising to start implementing full-fabrication testing with the new type of exposure tool.
